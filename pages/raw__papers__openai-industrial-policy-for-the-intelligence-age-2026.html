OpenAI, April 2026

Core thesis

OpenAI argues that the transition from today’s frontier AI to superintelligence is already underway and will require a new industrial policy designed to:

  1. Share prosperity broadly
  2. Mitigate risks
  3. Democratize access and agency

The paper frames this as a democratic governance challenge, not just a technical one: policy must keep pace so AI expands access, agency, and opportunity rather than concentrating wealth and power.

Key excerpts

  • “Now, we’re beginning a transition toward superintelligence: AI systems capable of outperforming the smartest humans even when they are assisted by AI.”
  • “At OpenAI, we believe we should navigate it through a democratic process that gives people real power to shape the AI future they want...”
  • “As capability scales, safety must scale with it.”
  • “The transition to superintelligence is not a distant possibility—it's already underway, and the choices we make in the near term will shape how its benefits and risks are distributed for decades to come.”
  • “AI data centers should pay their own way on energy so that households aren't subsidizing them; and they should generate local jobs and tax revenue.”
  • “This public-private collaboration should stave off regulatory capture and centralized control, instead preserving the freedom to innovate while ensuring that the onset of superintelligence isn't dominated by the most powerful forces in society.”

Executive summary notes

The paper argues that AI is moving from handling tasks that take minutes, to hours, and potentially months, which will transform work, institutions, and production. While OpenAI expects AI’s benefits to outweigh harms, it highlights serious risks:

  • job and industry disruption
  • misuse by bad actors
  • loss of alignment or control
  • anti-democratic government or institutional use
  • concentration of wealth and power

The proposed response is a new industrial policy agenda combining:

  • government tools: research funding, workforce development, regulation, procurement, investment
  • non-government experimentation: pilots, measurement, iteration
  • public-private collaboration without regulatory capture

The paper is exploratory, focused initially on the United States, but intended to support a global conversation.

Why a new industrial policy is needed

OpenAI compares the AI transition to prior technological shifts such as the Industrial Age, when democratic societies responded with labor protections, safety standards, social safety nets, and expanded access to education. The claim is that superintelligence will require an even more ambitious update to the social contract.

The paper says industrial policy is justified when:

  • technologies create opportunities and risks faster than institutions can adapt
  • breakthroughs need help becoming scaled industries
  • benefits would otherwise accrue narrowly

Section notes: building an open economy

Advanced AI could:

  • lower the cost of essential goods
  • accelerate scientific progress
  • increase productivity
  • free time for meaningful and relational life
  • help solve problems in medicine, food, agriculture, and energy

But it could also:

  • eliminate or transform jobs quickly
  • reshape industries unevenly
  • widen inequality
  • concentrate gains in a few firms
  • leave workers more productive without sharing the upside

One highlighted policy idea is worker perspectives: giving workers a formal voice in AI deployment decisions to improve job quality and safety while avoiding harmful uses that intensify workloads, reduce autonomy, or undermine fair scheduling and pay.
